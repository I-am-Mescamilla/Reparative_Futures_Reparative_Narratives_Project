Schools are closing nationwide. These are the common reasons we‚Äôre given:

Depopulation/Demographics

Jackson, like many other communities across the country, has schools that are not full. Some of the reasons why we don‚Äôt have enough kids to fill these classrooms anymore, in addition to unenrollment, include:

- Declining birth rates nationwide
- Declining/stagnating populations locally (this is experienced in both urban and rural communities!)

Budget Issues/Fiscal Savings

All of the previously mentioned factors work together and can worsen the ongoing budget issues of schools considered for closure. A smaller population provides less tax dollars for the operating budgets, as does unenrollment. Weaker, older facilities also often mean that making the necessary upgrades are more expensive than those needed by newer schools.


Some of the other reasons schools might be pushed towards closure due to budget concerns include:
- The belief that a larger, consolidated school is more cost-effective, as it requires fewer administrative workers for the number of students served (Lee and Lubienski, 2017)
- In addition to potential cost savings, the potential revenue generated by selling the building and/or its property (Bierbaum, 2021, p. 208)
- A feeling that there has been some sort of financial mismanagement that must be righted, even if the issues are simply a reflection of underfunding (De Witte and Van Klaveren, 2014)

What‚Äôs Happening Nationwide: Impacts of School Closures

The closure of a neighborhood‚Äôs school can have impacts much further reaching than simply where children get to go to school. The disruption and negative experiences of a school closure can grow into:
- Feeling detached from the community
- Losing belief in the political system
- Fear and harm to one‚Äôs social connections
- Negative impacts on displaced students‚Äô academic performance

Loss of Belief in Community & Political System

Just as schools can foster a sense of belonging, the loss of a school can lead to the sense of loss in a community.


- When school shutter, the public spaces they offered are taken away as well, removing community gathering spaces (Bierbaum, 2020) and health resources (Davis et al., 2023)
- Residents can lose trust in their public leaders, if they feel their needs have not been well accommodate for

Social Fears and Harm

Given that schools often provide more than just a physical amenity, but a sense of connection with other locals, the loss of such a space can result in a social disconnect for many. In the wake of a school closure, community members may experience, or have fears regarding the following


- Uncertainty of where to place loyalty;
- Whether or not to move on or to continue to advocate for a school once it‚Äôs been closed (Whitten et al., 2001)
- Students that had to transfer may not fit in socially at their new school, while parents of relocated students lose their parent-networks (Witten et al., 2001)
- Losing connection with the local community, due to longer transit times to farther schools (Lee and Lubienski, 2017)

Academic Loss

‚ÄúSchool closure increases the probability of high-school dropout between 49 and 68%‚Äù (Grau et al., 2018, p. 1). In addition to a higher likelihood of dropping out, there is also an association with lower academic performance levels in general, unless the students transferred to a significantly better performing school (Caven, 2019). These difficulties can be contributed to many factors, including:


- New, longer commutes
- New teachers and classmates
- Overcrowding in now too-full classrooms (Davis et al., 2023)

Inequities of Impacts & Cyclical/Continues Causal Trends

Amongst all of these impacts, there is a concentrating of the negative effects on already marginalized communities. More often than not, these impacts are felt most strongly by communities of color and lower-income families (Witten et al., 2001; Bierbaum, 2021; Lee and Lubienski, 2017). Without intentional interventions, the harm caused by school closure decisions may continue to worsen the quality of services and education accessible to marginalized students and their families.

Jackson Public Schools

Between 2017 and 2022, more than 5,000 schools closed nationwide, disrupting the lives of over 1.3 million students (National Center for Educational Statistics in Boehm, 2024, p.2). These closures don‚Äôt just affect classrooms‚Äîthey reshape neighborhoods, alter the built environment, and leave lasting marks on communities. While global forces like neoliberalism play a role (Thiem, 2009; Good, 2022, p. 606-607) local histories and policies often determine the school closure processes.

In Jackson Mississippi. 13 out of XX schools have closed, a staggering number that reflects decades of land-use policies, racial planning processes, and unjust development practices. Although limited in resources, the Jackson Public School District (JPS) is land rich. Yet discriminatory policies and predatory development have prevented JPS from stewarding land in ways that foster collective well-being for the District. Instead, value has been extracted from public assets at the expense of students and families.

School closures leave deep scars on communities‚Äîbut people in Jackson are asking the right question: What can be done to help our neighborhoods and students thrive? As the district prepares for facilities repurposing, the communities across the city must grapple with histories that have led to these closures to create futures in which the city can thrive. The Reparative Narratives, Reparative Futures Initiative aims to support these efforts by asking a critical question: How did we get here?

This webpage is a living archive, presenting histories and stories that weave together a powerful narrative of Jackson‚Äôs school closures. Through a reparative lens, we explore the policies and practices that shaped Jackson‚Äôs present-day challenges‚Äîwhile curating community voices that imagine a more equitable future for Jackson, one in which repair and justice is realized.
